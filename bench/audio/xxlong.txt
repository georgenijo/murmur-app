Murmur is designed from the ground up to keep every spoken word on your own machine, because dictation is one of the most personal forms of input a person can give to a computer. When you hold down the shortcut key and begin speaking, the audio never travels to a remote server, never touches a third party transcription service, and never lingers in a cloud bucket waiting to be indexed later. Instead, the recording flows directly into a local model that runs on your processor or your graphics hardware, turning your voice into text in a fraction of a second. The team behind the project believes that privacy should be the default rather than a premium feature you have to hunt for somewhere deep inside a settings menu. Every design decision, from the choice of an on device transducer to the clipboard first output strategy, follows from that single guiding principle. You can dictate a long email, a block of source code, a quick chat message to a colleague, or a quiet note to yourself, and none of it will ever leave the walls of your own computer. That guarantee is what makes the tool feel trustworthy enough to use for the small and ordinary thoughts you would never send to a stranger.
